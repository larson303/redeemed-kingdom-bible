The Name of Jesus

Discovering How Yehoshua Became Jesus

A Simple Question

Imagine you meet a Japanese woman named Yukiko (雪子). Her name means "Snow Child" in Japanese. But when you introduce her to your friends, you would not say, "This is Snow Child." You would say, "This is Yukiko." You would transliterate her name — preserving its sound — rather than translate its meaning.

This is standard practice in every language. Names are transliterated, not translated. We do this instinctively in daily life.

So here is a question worth sitting with: what is the original name of the person we call "Jesus"?

Most of us have simply never been shown the answer. It is not something typically taught in churches or Sunday schools, and there is no reason to feel bad about not knowing. But once you see it, it is hard to unsee — and the richness it unlocks is remarkable.

The Name Moses Gave

In Numbers 13:16, something significant happens. Moses is about to send spies into the Promised Land, and the text pauses to tell us:

"These are the names of the men whom Moses sent to spy out the land. Moses called Hoshea the son of Nun, Yehoshua."

— Numbers 13:16

Moses took the name Hoshea (הושע), which means "salvation," and placed the divine prefix Yah (יה) — the shortened form of Yahweh — at the beginning. The result was Yehoshua (יהושע), which means "Yahweh is salvation" or simply "Yah saves."

This is the name most English Bibles render as "Joshua" in the Old Testament.

And here is the remarkable part: it is the exact same name that the angel told Miriam (Mary) and Yosef (Joseph) to give their son in Matthew 1:21 — the name we read in English as "Jesus."

The angel said to name him Yehoshua "for he will save his people from their sins." That explanation only makes sense in Hebrew, because the name itself means "Yahweh saves." In English, there is no connection between the word "Jesus" and the concept of saving. But in the original language, the name is the prophecy.

How Did Yehoshua Become Jesus?

The name traveled through several languages over many centuries. At each step, the sounds shifted to fit the new language's rules. Here is the journey:

Stage Form Language What Changed
1. Original Yehoshua (יהושע) Hebrew The full name as given by Moses and the angel
2. Shortened Yeshua (ישוע) Aramaic / Late Hebrew A common shortened form, especially after the Babylonian exile — similar to how "William" becomes "Will"
3. Greek Iēsous (Ἰησοῦς) Koine Greek Greek has no "sh" sound, so it became "s." Greek masculine names end in -s, and the vowels shifted to fit Greek phonology
4. Latin Iesus Latin Carried over directly from the Greek form
5. English Jesus English The letter "J" was introduced around the 16th–17th century, replacing the original "I/Y" sound

None of these changes were made with bad intent. Each language did its best to carry the name forward. But by the time it reached English, almost nothing of the original sound remains. And with the sound, the embedded meaning — "Yahweh saves" — was lost to the average reader.

A Letter That Did Not Exist

Here is a fact that surprises many people: the letter J does not exist in Hebrew, Greek, or Latin.

The Hebrew alphabet has no J. The Greek alphabet has no J. Even the Latin alphabet, for most of its history, had no J. The letter emerged in European languages around the 16th century as a variant of the letter I.

This means that the 1611 King James Bible originally printed the name as "Iesus," not "Jesus." And it means that no one in the Bible — not his mother, not his disciples, not Paul — ever spoke the sound "Jee-zus." They knew him as Yehoshua or Yeshua.

This is not controversial among scholars. It is simply a fact of linguistic history that most people have never had reason to encounter.

Similarly, the letter V as we know it did not exist in ancient Hebrew. The sixth letter of the Hebrew alphabet (ו) is debated among scholars — some render it as waw (a "w" sound) and others as vav (a "v" sound). This is why you may see "Yahweh" spelled differently in various sources. The original pronunciation was most likely closer to a "w" sound.

A Pattern Hidden in Plain Sight

Once you start looking, you notice something fascinating: many of the most important names in Scripture contain a reference to Yah (Yahweh) — but you would never know it from the English versions. Scholars call these theophoric names: names that carry the name of God within them.

Here are just a few examples:

Original Hebrew Familiar English Meaning
Yehoshua (יהושע) Joshua / Jesus Yahweh is salvation
Eliyahu (אליהו) Elijah My God is Yahweh
Yeshayahu (ישעיהו) Isaiah Salvation of Yahweh
YirmeYahu (ירמיהו) Jeremiah Yahweh will exalt
ZecharYahu (זכריהו) Zechariah Yahweh remembers
Mattityahu (מתתיהו) Matthew Gift of Yahweh
Yahuchanan (יהוחנן) John Yahweh is gracious
Yahudah (יהודה) Judah Praise of Yahweh

Do you see the pattern? In the original Hebrew, these names are practically singing. Yahweh is salvation. My God is Yahweh. Salvation of Yahweh. Yahweh remembers. Gift of Yahweh. Yahweh is gracious. When you read the prophets in Hebrew, the name of God is woven into the very names of the people who speak for Him.

In English, all of that is invisible.

How did this happen? Over time, a tradition developed within Jewish scribal practice of avoiding the divine name out of deep reverence. This reverence was sincere — but it gradually extended beyond just the name Yahweh itself to the "Yah" element within people's names. As these names passed through Greek and Latin, the connection was further obscured. By the time they reached English, the divine thread that ran through them was completely hidden.

What God Said About His Name

With all of this in mind, it is worth revisiting what God Himself said about His name. In Exodus 3:15, He speaks directly to Moses:

"You shall tell the children of Israel this, Yahweh, the God of your fathers, the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob, has sent me to you. This is my name forever, and this is my memorial to all generations."

— Exodus 3:15

"Forever" and "to all generations." Those are strong words. God did not say His name was temporary, or that a future tradition should replace it with a title. He said this is His name — forever.

And yet, most English Bibles print "the LORD" (in small capitals) wherever the Hebrew text reads "Yahweh." This practice is so universal that many lifelong Bible readers have never realized that a specific, personal name is being replaced by a generic title.

The scribes who began this practice were not trying to dishonor God. They were trying to protect His name from being spoken carelessly. Their hearts were in the right place. But over centuries, a protective tradition hardened into doctrine, and the result is that the very name God said should be remembered "to all generations" became the name that almost no one knows.

The prophet Hosea speaks to this tension beautifully:

"It will be in that day," says Yahweh, "that you will call me 'my husband' (Ishi), and will no longer call me 'my master' (Baali)."

— Hosea 2:16

The Hebrew word Baal means "lord" or "master." God draws a distinction between being known by name in a relationship of love, and being addressed by a title of authority. He wants to be known — not just acknowledged.

"I Have Come in My Father's Name"

In John 5:43, Yehoshua (Jesus) makes a statement that takes on new depth when you understand the Hebrew:

"I have come in my Father's name."

— John 5:43

In English, this reads as a metaphor — he came with his Father's authority, representing his Father. And that is true. But in Hebrew, it is also literally true. The name Yehoshua contains Yah — the name of the Father. He came in his Father's name. The name of the Father is embedded in the name of the Son.

This is the kind of richness that gets lost when names are translated rather than transliterated. It is not that the English Bible is "wrong" — it is that there are layers of meaning in the original language that the English simply cannot carry without some help.

What the Redeemed Kingdom Bible Does

The word "Redeemed" in the name of this Bible is intentional. We are not claiming to have created something new. We are working to redeem the text from human traditions that, however well-intentioned, have obscured what God originally revealed. To redeem something is to buy it back, to restore it to its rightful state. That is what this translation aims to do — not to innovate, but to recover.

We are not asking anyone to stop saying "Jesus." That name has carried the faith of billions of people for centuries, and God has honored it. What we are doing is opening a window into what was originally there, so that readers who are curious can see it for themselves.

The Redeemed Kingdom Bible adds parenthetical transliterations alongside the familiar English names. On the first appearance of a name in each book, you will see something like:

  • Jesus (Yehoshua) — "Yahweh is salvation"
  • Joshua (Yehoshua) — the same name as Jesus
  • Elijah (Eliyahu) — "My God is Yahweh"
  • Isaiah (Yeshayahu) — "Salvation of Yahweh"
  • Matthew (Mattityahu) — "Gift of Yahweh"
  • John (Yahuchanan) — "Yahweh is gracious"

The goal is not to make the Bible harder to read. It is to restore what was always there — gently, alongside the familiar text — so that you can begin to hear what the original readers heard.

When you read the prophets and realize that their very names declare who sent them — when you see that the Messiah's name carries the name of his Father — when the phrase "Yahweh saves" echoes every time you read "Jesus" — the text comes alive in a way it simply cannot in English alone.

His name is Yehoshua. It means "Yahweh saves." And that name was given for a reason.

Summary

  • The original Hebrew name is Yehoshua (יהושע), meaning "Yahweh is salvation"
  • Moses created this name by adding Yah to Hoshea (Numbers 13:16)
  • It is the same name rendered as "Joshua" in the OT and "Jesus" in the NT
  • The name was shortened to Yeshua in Aramaic, then became Iēsous in Greek, Iesus in Latin, and finally Jesus in English
  • The letter J did not exist in Hebrew, Greek, or Latin — it appeared in English around the 16th–17th century
  • Many Biblical names contain "Yah" (Yahweh) — a connection hidden by centuries of translation
  • God declared that Yahweh is His name forever (Exodus 3:15)
  • The Messiah's name literally means "Yahweh saves" — the name is the prophecy
