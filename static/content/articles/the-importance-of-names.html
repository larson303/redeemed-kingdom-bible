The Importance of Names

Why Names Matter in Scripture

Names Are Not Accidents

In modern Western culture, we often choose names because we like how they sound. We might name a child "Olivia" or "Ethan" without thinking much about the meaning behind the name. But in the ancient Hebrew world, names were not decorative. They were declarations.

A name could express a parent's hope, mark a moment in history, describe a person's character, or — most remarkably — carry a prophetic message from God. Throughout Scripture, names are treated as something weighty, something intentional, something that means something.

Once you begin to see this, the Bible reads differently. Names you may have skimmed over for years suddenly light up with meaning.

When God Changes a Name

One of the most striking patterns in Scripture is that God Himself renames people at pivotal moments. These are not casual nicknames. Each new name signals a new identity, a new calling, or a new covenant relationship. Consider:

If names did not matter, why would God bother changing them?

Names That Tell a Story

Beyond the renamings, many names in Scripture were given with specific, intentional meaning at the moment of birth or calling. Here are a few examples:

  • Adam (אדם) — from adamah, meaning "earth" or "ground." The first man was formed from the earth, and his name says so.
  • Eve / Chava (חוה) — "life-giver" or "living." Adam named her this because she would be the mother of all the living (Genesis 3:20).
  • Noah / Noach (נח) — "rest" or "comfort." His father Lamech named him this saying, "This one will comfort us" (Genesis 5:29).
  • Isaac / Yitzchaq (יצחק) — "he laughs." Both Abraham and Sarah laughed when God promised them a son in their old age (Genesis 17:17, 18:12). God told them to name the child "he laughs" — turning their disbelief into a permanent reminder of joy.
  • Samuel / Shemuel (שמואל) — "heard by God." Hannah prayed desperately for a child, and when God answered, she named him "God heard" (1 Samuel 1:20).
  • Immanuel / ImmanuYah (עמנואל) — "God with us." A prophetic name given by Isaiah declaring that God would dwell among His people (Isaiah 7:14).

These are not trivia. These names carry the emotional, historical, and theological weight of the moment they were given. When you read them with their meanings, the narrative deepens in a way that English alone cannot convey.

Names That Carry the Name of God

Perhaps the most remarkable category of Biblical names are theophoric names — names that contain a reference to God within them. In Hebrew, these typically include Yah (יה), Yahu (יהו), or El (אל) as part of the name.

These names are everywhere in Scripture. The prophets, the kings, the disciples — their names are declarations about God:

When you read the prophets and realize that their very names declare who sent them, the text takes on a richness that is simply invisible in English. Isaiah is not just a name — it is a statement: "Salvation of Yahweh." And that is exactly what his book is about.

The tragedy is that centuries of translation have stripped these names of their meaning. The "Yah" and "Yahu" elements — the very syllables that connect these names to God — have been smoothed away through Greek, Latin, and English rendering. Most readers have no idea that the names they read every Sunday are singing about Yahweh.

Transliteration vs. Translation

In linguistics, there is a clear distinction between transliteration and translation:

  • Transliteration preserves the sound of a word when moving it between languages. The goal is to let the reader hear what the original speaker heard.
  • Translation converts the meaning of a word into the target language.

For common words, translation is appropriate. The Hebrew word mayim (מים) should be translated as "water" in English — no one would benefit from reading "mayim" in an English Bible.

But names are different. Names are supposed to be transliterated. When someone named Yukiko moves to the United States, she does not become "Snow Child." She is still Yukiko. When someone named Pierre visits Japan, he is not called by the Japanese word for "rock." He is still Pierre.

And yet, for Biblical names, we have done exactly what we would never do in everyday life. We have translated, shortened, filtered through Greek and Latin phonology, and produced names that bear little resemblance to what was originally spoken. "Yehoshua" became "Jesus." "Yaacob" became "James." "Yahuchanan" became "John." "Miriam" became "Mary."

None of this was done with bad intent. Each language did the best it could with the sounds available to it. But the cumulative result is that the original names — and the meanings embedded in them — have been hidden from most readers for centuries.

Names in the New Testament

It is worth noting that the early followers of Yehoshua (Jesus) continued this tradition of meaningful naming. Consider:

  • Barnabas — Acts 4:36 tells us his real name was Joseph, but the apostles called him Barnabas, meaning "Son of Encouragement." His new name described his character.
  • Peter / Kepha — Simon was renamed by Yehoshua himself. "Kepha" means "rock" in Aramaic. The new name was prophetic: this man would become the bedrock of the early community of believers.
  • Saul / Paul — While scholars debate whether this was a formal renaming, the shift from his Hebrew name Saul (Sha'ul, "asked for") to his Roman name Paul (Paulus, "small/humble") coincides with his transformation from persecutor to apostle.

The pattern is consistent from Genesis to Revelation: names carry weight. They are chosen with care, changed with purpose, and loaded with meaning. This is not a minor theme in Scripture. It is woven into the fabric of the story.

What Has Been Lost

When we read English translations, we are reading names that have been filtered through multiple languages and centuries of tradition. In most cases, the meaning that was obvious to the original audience is completely invisible to us.

Imagine reading a story where every character's name tells you something about them — where the hero's name means "Yahweh saves," the prophet's name means "Salvation of Yahweh," and the place where God dwells carries His name within it. That is what the Hebrew Bible is. But in English, it reads like a list of unfamiliar syllables with no apparent meaning.

This is not anyone's fault. It is simply the result of a long journey through many languages. But it is something we can begin to recover.

What the Redeemed Kingdom Bible Does

The Redeemed Kingdom Bible adds parenthetical transliterations and meanings alongside the familiar English names. This allows you to:

  • See the original name as it was spoken in Hebrew
  • Hear the connection to God in theophoric names that contain "Yah" or "El"
  • Understand the meaning that was obvious to the original audience
  • Recognize patterns — like how many prophets carry God's name within their own

We are not replacing the familiar names. We are restoring what was always there alongside them. The goal is to redeem these names from centuries of tradition that, however well-meaning, have hidden their beauty and their purpose.

God is a God who names with intention. He renamed Abram to declare a covenant. He renamed Jacob to mark a transformation. He gave His own Son a name that literally means "Yahweh saves." When we recover the meaning of these names, we are not adding to Scripture. We are uncovering what was always there.
